

Key dates in the unfolding rivalry of Russia, China and the United States in Central Asia

Recent developments hinting at China's strategy to increase its influence in Central Asia and work against the U.S. presence in the region:
-May 25: China gives red-carpet welcome to Uzbekistan President Islam Karimov just days after his government's bloody crackdown on protesters kills hundreds;
-May 25: The China National Petroleum Corporation and the Uzbekistan National Oil and Gas Corp. sign a joint venture deal worth $600 million;
-July 4: "In response to the recent serious changes in the regional and international situation," Chinese President Hu Jintao signs strategic partnership agreement with Nursultan Nazarbayev, president of energy-rich Kazakhstan. At the signing in the Kazakh capital, Astana, Nazarbayev praises China's "growing international clout."
-July 5: A regional alliance led by China and Russia calls for the United States and its coalition allies in Afghanistan to set a date for withdrawing from bases in Central Asia.
-July 11: Kyrgyzstan's new president, Kurmanbek Bakiyev says that since the Afghanistan situation has stabilized, "now we may begin discussing the necessity of U.S. military forces' presence." But on July 26, Kyrgyz officials assure Defense Secretary Donald H. Rumsfeld that U.S. forces face no near-term deadline for withdrawing from an air base near the Kyrgyz capital;
-July 29: Uzbek government tells the United States it must withdraw its forces from the Karshi-Khanabad air base within 180 days.
-Aug. 2: China and Russia, once ideological and military foes, announce they will holding an unprecedented joint military exercise in China Aug. 18-25, using long-range bombers and submarines to settle an imaginary conflict in a foreign land.
